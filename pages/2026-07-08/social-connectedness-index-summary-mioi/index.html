The Social Connectedness Index

A teaching summary of Johnston, Kuchler, Kulkarni, and Stroebel's 2026 paper on measuring real-world social ties between places using aggregated Facebook friendship links.

Paper: 6 pages Data: Facebook friendship links Coverage: 178 countries Focus: places, not income classes

One-Sentence Takeaway

The Social Connectedness Index, or SCI, measures how socially tied two geographic places are by asking: after adjusting for how many Facebook users live in each place, how likely is a user in place i to be Facebook friends with a user in place j?

Plain English: SCI is a map of friendship intensity between places. A higher SCI means people in two places are unusually likely to be friends, relative to the size of their Facebook-user populations.

The Basic Idea

Suppose you want to know whether Los Angeles is more socially connected to New York, Mexico City, Seoul, or a nearby California county. Counting raw Facebook friendships is not enough, because large places mechanically have more people and therefore more possible friendships.

The SCI fixes this by normalizing the number of friendship links by the number of Facebook users in each location. That turns the measure into something like a relative friendship probability.

SCIi,j = Friendshipsi,j / (Popi x Popj)
Friendships are Facebook friendship links between locations; Pop is the Facebook-user population in each location.

If the SCI between A and B is twice the SCI between A and C, then a randomly chosen Facebook user in A is roughly twice as likely to be friends with a randomly chosen Facebook user in B as with one in C. The key word is relative: the published values are scaled inside each dataset, so ratios and rankings are the meaningful interpretation.

What the Paper Contributes

This paper describes a new release of the SCI data as of January 25, 2026. Compared with earlier versions, it expands global coverage and adds finer geographic resolution.

Broader global coverage The sample includes Facebook users from 178 countries who were recently active and had more than 20 friends.
More granular places The release supports country-to-country, region-to-region, and country-to-region connectedness.
Privacy protections Small geographic units are excluded and published statistics are protected with differential privacy.
Research-ready stability The authors report a 0.97 correlation with the 2021 release at both country and U.S. county levels.

What Data Go Into It?

The paper uses Facebook friendships as a proxy for offline social ties. A Facebook friendship is useful for this purpose because it is undirected and requires mutual confirmation by both users.

  • User sample: active Facebook users in the 30 days before January 25, 2026.
  • Friendship threshold: users must have more than 20 Facebook friends.
  • Location assignment: users are assigned to estimated home locations using signals such as self-reported location, IP addresses, and platform activity patterns.
  • Minimum geography size: units with fewer than 500 users are excluded.
  • Privacy: released statistics use differential privacy, so researchers work with aggregate protected data, not individual-level friendship records.

The authors are careful that Facebook is not a perfect map of all social relationships. It is large enough to be unusually useful, but not universal or identical across countries.

How to Interpret a Map

Pick one focal location. The SCI map colors other places by how strongly they are connected to that focal location. Darker or higher values mean a stronger relative likelihood of friendship.

Pattern What it means Why it matters
Distance decay Nearby places usually have stronger ties. Social networks are still geographically grounded.
Border effects Connections often drop at state or national borders, even after accounting for distance. Political and administrative boundaries shape social life.
Historical ties Migration, settlement, and trade patterns can leave visible network traces. Today's social ties can encode older economic and cultural relationships.
Network concentration Some places' networks are highly local; others are spread across many regions. Different places may be exposed to different information, opportunities, shocks, and norms.

What This Is Useful For

The SCI is useful whenever the researcher thinks relationships between places matter, but geographic distance alone is too crude.

  • Migration and diaspora ties
  • International trade and regional commerce
  • Spread of beliefs, behaviors, or health information
  • Disaster response and displacement prediction
  • Housing markets and location choices
  • Access to capital and investor networks
  • Policy diffusion across regions
  • Community clustering and regional identity
Teaching example: If two regions are far apart but have high SCI, distance alone would miss something important. That "something" might be a migration corridor, shared language, colonial history, trade relationship, or another social bridge.

SCI vs. Economic Connectedness

This is the distinction that matters for the earlier Opportunity Insights question.

Concept Question it answers Unit of comparison Typical interpretation
Social Connectedness Index How socially tied are two places? Geographic locations People in place A are more or less likely to be friends with people in place B.
Economic connectedness How often do lower-SES and higher-SES people become friends? Income or socioeconomic groups Low-income people have more or fewer high-income friends.

Both concepts use friendship-network data, but they study different dimensions. SCI is about where social ties go. Economic connectedness is about who is connected across class lines.

Limitations to Remember

  • Facebook users are not everyone. Coverage varies by country, internet access, age, and platform adoption.
  • A friendship link is not tie strength. SCI counts whether a friendship link exists, not how often people interact or how emotionally close they are.
  • Norms differ across places. Some cultures or age groups may add Facebook friends more broadly than others.
  • It is relative, not causal by itself. SCI can reveal network structure, but additional research design is needed to claim that connectedness caused an outcome.
  • Aggregation requires care. Combining small regions into larger regions should use population-weighted aggregation, not a simple average.

How to Explain It to Someone Else

  1. Start with the problem: social ties matter, but normal datasets rarely measure them at large scale.
  2. Define the object: SCI measures friendship intensity between geographic places.
  3. Explain the normalization: raw friendship counts are divided by the product of the two Facebook-user populations.
  4. Give the ratio interpretation: twice the SCI means about twice the likelihood of friendship between random users from those places.
  5. State the caveat: this is Facebook-based, aggregate, privacy-protected, and relative.

Quick Self-Check

Why not just count friendships? Big places have more possible friendships. SCI adjusts for the number of Facebook users in both places.
What does "twice as high" mean? A random user in the focal place is about twice as likely to be friends with a random user in the comparison place.
Does SCI measure class mixing? No. That is economic connectedness. SCI measures geographic connectedness.
Is it individual-level data? No. The public release is aggregate and privacy-protected.
