01

The bet in one paragraph

Every client you've ever closed arrived warm — through YouTube, X, talks, and referrals. SEO is therefore not your acquisition engine, and this plan doesn't pretend it is. Instead it does two cheaper things: (A) makes the website excellent at the job it already has — convincing buyers who found you elsewhere and are quietly vetting you — and (B) runs a low-effort experiment: every piece of content you already make gets an economist-framed written twin on the site, targeting real search queries and, just as importantly, being quotable by ChatGPT and Claude. The experiment has a hard checkpoint in January 2027. If the data says it isn't working, part B freezes and you've lost almost nothing.

03

The two-part strategy

Part A — The vetting surface

The only mechanism with evidence behind it: buyers who found you elsewhere check the site before booking. So the site gets what those buyers look for:

  • Public syllabus pages for every delivered workshop. SEACEN literally asked for your Bundesbank syllabus before inquiring — that artifact closes deals. Naming is permission-gated per client: named / anonymized ("a European central bank research centre") / withheld, read from your engagement ledger, never re-decided by the site.
  • Client proof on the trainings page — institutions served, testimonials where permitted.
  • A "how did you hear about me?" question on TidyCal bookings, with distinct Google/search and ChatGPT/AI assistant options. No webhooks, no Zapier — a scheduled script pulls answers via the TidyCal API (or parses the booking emails already in your Gmail) and logs them.
  • Basic hygiene — articles finally in the sitemap (they aren't today), canonical URLs and structured data (the site has none anywhere).

Part B — The content-twin pipeline

The policy in one sentence: never write an SEO article from scratch; give a written twin to content you already made. You have 53 written video scripts and ~25 substantive X threads with no written counterpart. Twins are agent-drafted, economist-framed, titled to match a verified query, and end with two calls to action (newsletter, trainings). Your job is a weekly digest costing ≤15 minutes.

Twins come in two formats, and both are MDX files published by pull request. Most are simple prose articles — cheap by design, but with the full component vocabulary available (a D3 figure or prompt block can appear in an ordinary twin). Flagship pillars get the guide treatment: an Every-style block system (copyable prompt blocks, callouts, comparison tables, a section rail) in your own visual language. Same architecture as your workshop slide kit — a template library plus a full-custom escape hatch.

Why "economist lens on everything," when general-dev framing would get more traffic? Because the traffic isn't the point — the buyer is. And because the second audience for these pages isn't Google at all: it's answer engines. When a research director asks ChatGPT "who trains economists on agentic coding," LLMs cite written pages, not videos. Right now your best teaching is invisible to them.

What was deliberately rejected

A dedicated keyword-chasing content operation (the "just chase the arbitrage" option). The traffic skews general-developer, it competes for your time against channels that demonstrably close $20k deals, and comparison queries are precisely what AI Overviews are eating. If that business is ever worth building, it's AI MBA's lane — not this brand's.

04

How the twin pipeline works

The machinery lives in a small repo-local ledger (SQLite, modeled on your newsletter-sources store) — not in Convex, and with no admin UI. Three tables: sources (every video and qualifying post, with status), twins (each article's lifecycle state), and a keyword registry (one canonical article per query, across both brands, so two pages never cannibalize the same search intent).

Seeding first — and written content becomes files

The pipeline's very first run ingests everything already published on both sites as claimed sources and keywords — a Convex audit found 7 econ articles and 13 tutorials live in the database, roughly half the backfill queue among them. Most exist only as database rows, which is exactly why earlier inventory passes missed them: agents see files by default, not databases. The fix went all the way: articles and tutorials convert to MDX files in git (same slugs, same URLs), shortcodes become components, and Convex retires for written content — keeping only what's genuinely dynamic (courses, videos, subscribers, the gated-unlock check). Publishing becomes a pull request: merge → Vercel deploys in ~3 minutes, every draft gets a preview URL, and a broken component fails the build instead of a reader's page load.

The lifecycle of a twin

candidate→ screened→ keyword_verified→ in_review→ published
exits along the way: excludednews / no script / guest / low engagement keyword_blockedquery dead or claimed, no re-target
from review: rejected stale>4 weeks unreviewed → keyword re-verified before it can proceed

The screening rules, precisely

Which videos qualify?
Every new published video that has a script. (Your production pipeline writes one for essentially every video.)
Which X posts qualify?
Evergreen teaching content that is either a structured how-to (any engagement level) or clears 100 likes.
What about a viral news post — say, a Fable review with 1,000 likes?
Excluded. The news rule hard-overrides the engagement rule. Model-release reactions decay in weeks; twins are for content that's still true next year.
What if the target keyword died or got claimed by the time we draft?
Re-target to the nearest verified unclaimed query. If none exists, the article can still publish flagged "LLM-citability only" — no keyword claim, still quotable by answer engines.
What happens when I get lazy and skip three weeks?
Nothing piles up. At most 5 drafts can be in flight; anything unreviewed for 4 weeks flips to stale and must re-verify its keyword. Skipped weeks are absorbed, not accumulated — and if digests go unreviewed for 6+ weeks, that's checkpoint evidence in itself.
Does this ever become fully automatic?
The ratchet is defined now, built later: after 10 consecutive approvals without substantive edits, the pipeline proposes auto-publish — still gated on a verified, unclaimed keyword and both CTAs present.

05

What gets built, in what order

Ten units across three phases. The ordering principle: measurement first — every week without attribution capture shortens the data window the January decision depends on.

Phase A — Measurement foundation & vetting surface

Serves the buyers you already have; starts the checkpoint's data clock.

U1

Booking-source attribution: TidyCal intake question + scheduled sweep into the relationship ledger. No dependencies — do first.

U2

SEO hygiene: articles/tutorials/trainings into the sitemap, canonical URLs, structured data, title audit.

U3

Public syllabus pages (permission-gated) + "Past trainings" proof on the trainings page. After U2.

U4

Trainings CTA component (shared MDX vocabulary) + the twin article template convention.

Phase B — Twin pipeline

The machinery that makes twins a byproduct instead of a project.

U5

Content ledger + keyword registry (SQLite + small CLI). Test-first on state transitions and one-article-per-query enforcement.

U10

MDX conversion: all ~20 published articles + tutorials become .mdx files in git (same URLs, no redirects); shortcodes become components; the gated-unlock flow moves server-side (the one risk-bearing piece, test-first); Convex retires for written content. Independent; unblocks U6/U7.

U6

The pipeline skill + weekly cadence: detection → screening → keyword claim → drafting chain (tutorial-writing / article-writing / your voice skill / illustrations) → digest with Vercel preview links → approve by merging the PR. After U4, U5, U10.

U9

Guide surface: file-based MDX shell (section rail + ~640px column, econ-brand typography) with a five-block vocabulary — prose, copy-prompt, callout, comparison table, CTA. Every-style mechanics, your visual language. Public-only: no gating, no educli, no DB. After U2.

Phase C — Backfill & checkpoint

Spend the surplus; make the January decision mechanical.

U7

Execute the 15-item backfill queue through the pipeline, free wins first. After U2, U4, U5, U6.

U8

Checkpoint instrumentation: the PostHog metric, the baseline snapshot (Aug 1), the decision table, the quarterly LLM-visibility check, a scheduled reminder. After U1, U2.

07

Measurement & the January decision

The brainstorm's most important honest admission was yours: "this could very well be a waste of time." So the plan makes quitting cheap and the decision mechanical. Two inputs, tracked from launch:

  • Attributed inquiries — any booking whose intake answer says Google/search or ChatGPT/AI assistant.
  • The traffic metric — monthly non-branded organic sessions: search-engine referrals landing on content pages (articles, tutorials, guides, trainings), homepage excluded so branded name-searches don't flatter the number. Baseline snapshotted August 1.

≥1 attributed inquiry · trend up

Continue and scale. Both mechanisms firing — the bet paid.

≥1 attributed inquiry · flat traffic

Continue, narrowed. Only high-intent lanes: comparisons, course/training, claude-code-for-research.

0 inquiries · metric ≥2× baseline, 2 consecutive months

Continue. Traffic compounding; give conversion more time.

0 inquiries · below threshold

Freeze part B. Twins stop. Syllabi, proof, attribution capture, and published articles stay live — part A was never conditional.

Alongside: a quarterly LLM-visibility check — run buyer-shaped prompts ("who trains economists on agentic coding?") against ChatGPT and Claude and log whether you're cited. Treated as directional, not decisive.

08

Seven open design decisions

The review pass (four reviewer agents: coherence, feasibility, design, scope) plus the published-content audit left seven items for your judgment before or during implementation. Only #7 blocks anything (the retrofit half of the backfill); all are cheap to decide now and annoying to discover later.

  1. Where does the ledger database live? The plan says "in the repo" but its own precedent (newsletter-sources) stores host-external. A repo-committed SQLite forks state across git worktrees and Ralph loops. Recommendation: host-external at ~/.local/share/ai_mba/content_twins.sqlite. feasibility · proposed fix
  2. Commit to the {{cta:trainings}} shortcode form. The bare {{trainings}} spelling won't match the existing shortcode regex and would silently render nothing. feasibility · proposed fix
  3. Syllabus-page booking CTA: link, not modal. Linking to /trainings keeps syllabus pages static and the TidyCal modal centralized in one place. design · proposed fix
  4. How does an anonymized syllabus page stay credible? The testimonials strip names people at named institutions — rendering it on an anonymized page leaks the client. Needs a defined proof model (role-without-affiliation quotes, audience/format/outcome facts). design · decision
  5. Define the syllabus page's own structure. The guide template it "reuses" is video-guide-shaped; without a stated section order (audience → curriculum → outcomes → proof → CTA), two implementers build two different pages. design · decision
  6. Trim structured data to what the checkpoint justifies? Article schema + canonical serve LLM-citability directly; Organization/Service/Course schemas on the trainings surface are speculative build-out ahead of a freezable bet. scope · decision
  7. Which brand keeps a contested query? Windows install, 1M-context, and the Git guide are live on ai-mba.io; the twin policy is econ-lens. Duplicating them splits authority across two low-authority domains in the same SERP. Proposed default: the brand already publishing keeps its query; econ claims only research-angled variants and cross-links; moving a page between brands requires an explicit call plus a redirect. content audit · decision — resolve before the retrofit batch

09

Guardrails and honest risks

  • Indexing lag is real. A near-zero-authority domain takes months to rank even at difficulty 5–15. The January thresholds are set against a tiny base on purpose — expect small absolute numbers.
  • Keywords are volatile. Several targets move at triple-digit yearly rates; every twin re-verifies its query at draft time, and some queue items may land as citability-only pieces.
  • TidyCal API coverage is unverified. If intake answers aren't exposed, the Gmail-parse fallback carries the unit. Confirm your plan tier includes custom intake questions (paid plans only).
  • The gated-article unlock flow is the MDX conversion's one dangerous edge. It feeds newsletter capture, so it gets test-first treatment and the database path stays read-only until every migrated page passes a parity check.
  • The "weekly automation" is honest about its limits. A cron can't run a chat-approval loop; in practice the cadence is a scheduled agent preparing the digest and you spending your 15 minutes. That's the design, not a failure of it.
  • Your laziness is priced in. The WIP cap, staleness TTL, and digest exist because you said you'd eventually slack. If the loop still dies, the checkpoint reads that as data — not as guilt.
